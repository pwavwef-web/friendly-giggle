## Post Text

In 2019, Ghana welcomed over 1.1 million international visitors — a record at the time. The Year of Return campaign played a central role. But what happened to the momentum?

As part of my Contemporary Issues in Tourism (TMG406) coursework at UCC, I've been examining the long-term impact of Ghana's diaspora tourism strategy. Here's what stands out:

**What the Year of Return achieved:**
— Over 200,000 African-American visitors made the journey in 2019 alone
— Tourism revenue hit approximately $3.3 billion — Ghana's highest recorded
— International media coverage amplified Ghana's profile across the US, UK, and the Caribbean
— "Beyond the Return" launched as a 10-year follow-up framework in 2020

**The harder questions:**
— How much of that revenue reached local communities vs. large hotel chains?
— Were craft vendors, local guides, and community guesthouses part of the value chain?
— Has the momentum survived COVID-19 and global travel disruptions?

Ghana's government got the marketing right. The destination brand resonated. But sustainable diaspora tourism needs inclusive supply chains — not just impressive arrival numbers.

For my dissertation (TMG499), this intersection of heritage tourism and economic inclusion is one of the most compelling areas I'm exploring.

What's your view — can diaspora tourism be a genuine poverty-reduction tool, or is it primarily a middle-class and luxury market?
